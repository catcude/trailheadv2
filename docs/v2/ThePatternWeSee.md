# 03 The Pattern We See

There is a pattern I have seen again and again. I have seen it in students sitting in classrooms, trying to understand why learning seems easier for everyone else or why ‘perfection’ doesn’t make the anxiety go away. I have seen it in teenagers who are being asked to decide who they are and what they want to become before they have had enough life to understand how they work. I have seen it in the troubled kid everyone knows has potential, but no one can quite seem to reach for any meaningful length of time. I have seen it in parents trying desperately to help their children succeed while wondering whether they are pushing too hard or not hard enough. I have seen it in adults who followed the path they were told would lead somewhere good, only to arrive and realize it never quite felt like their own.

This pattern was familiar because I, too, had lived it.

We ask people to make enormous decisions about their lives before helping them understand the person making those decisions. We measure performance before exploring conditions. We label outcomes before looking for patterns. We tell young people to find their passion, choose a career, build a future, or discover their true path, as though the right answer is somewhere out there waiting to be found. But a person is not a blank slate choosing among a set of predetermined roads. Every person arrives with a unique combination of tendencies, capacities, sensitivities, experiences, interests, strengths, challenges, and ways of engaging with the world. These things are not destiny, and they do not determine who someone will become. But they are information, and information becomes powerful when we learn how to notice it.

The problem is that most of us were never taught to look. Instead, we learned to ask different questions: Am I smart enough? Am I good at this? Do I like this enough to continue on this path? What should I be when I grow up? What is the right choice? Why can’t I do what everyone else seems able to do? What is wrong with me? But even some of these seemingly simple questions are harder to answer than we pretend. How can a young person know what they enjoy, what they are capable of, or where they might thrive when their experience of the world is still so limited? We often ask people to draw conclusions about themselves from the small fraction of life they have had the chance to encounter. And when we do not yet have enough experience to know ourselves, it is natural to look to other people for answers. We begin to rely on parents, teachers, peers, grades, praise, criticism, popularity, expectations, and comparison to tell us who we are and what we are good at. Over time, those questions can pull us farther and farther away from more useful ones. What conditions help me thrive? What gives me energy? What drains it? What patterns keep appearing in my life? What comes naturally to me? What do I keep returning to? What have I learned through difficulty? What kind of person am I becoming?

This pattern is the tendency to orient ourselves around external validation. In other words, shaping our behavior based on approval, rewards, or avoidance of disapproval rather than developing an internal sense of direction. It often becomes most noticeable in kids who do not respond strongly to those usual forms of external validation. They are often the inconvenient ones: the students who are not particularly motivated by grades, praise, approval, gold stars, disappointed looks, prizes, or the threat of getting in trouble. Their behavior may look rude, disrespectful, lazy, defiant, or apathetic on the surface.

I am not suggesting that harmful behavior should be encouraged or tolerated. Boundaries matter. Accountability matters. Learning how our actions affect other people matters. But we should be careful not to confuse the way a trait is currently being expressed with the value of the trait itself. It took me a long time to understand this in my own life. The part of me that genuinely did not care what other people thought was also the part of me that could do almost anything I truly decided to do. I did not need permission, consensus, or everyone around me to understand. That part of me could be reckless, stubborn, and difficult. It could create conflict. But it was also a source of enormous strength. For years, I thought I needed to suppress that part of myself. What I really needed was to learn how to use it well. I did not need to lose my independence; I needed to mature it. Defiance could become courage. Stubbornness could become perseverance. Disregard for approval could become freedom. A refusal to wait for permission could become the willingness to act when something mattered.

I see the same potential in young people. Some of the students who are hardest for systems to manage may also be the least likely to build their lives around pleasing those systems. That can create real problems while they are still developing judgment, self-regulation, responsibility, and an understanding of how their choices affect others. But it can also contain the beginnings of extraordinary agency. Too often, we respond only to the inconvenience. The child resists, refuses, stops trying, gets written up, is removed from class, sent to an alternative setting, pushed toward a GED, or quietly placed in the category of one of the bad kids. Once a young person begins to believe that is who they are, things can get worse before they get better.

Behavior is one thing. Identity is another. A young person who believes, “I made a bad choice,” still has somewhere to go. A young person who believes, “I am bad,” may begin to build a life around proving everyone right. I know how real that can feel because I remember being fifteen and genuinely believing my life was over. I did not think I was simply going through a difficult season. I felt too far gone. To an adult looking back, fifteen can seem impossibly young, with an entire life still ahead. But from inside that experience, the world you know can feel like the whole world. School, social status, failure, and the labels placed on you can all feel permanent. Without enough distance or experience to understand that these judgments are not destiny, a young person may simply begin to believe them.

That is why we need to be careful about what we assume we are seeing in a difficult child. Defiance can contain independence. Resistance can contain discernment. Stubbornness can become perseverance. A refusal to conform can become originality. None of that means every expression of those traits is healthy. It means the work is not always to erase the trait, but to help the person understand it, develop it, and use it responsibly. Some children do not need to become more compliant versions of themselves. They need help becoming more skillful, responsible, self-aware versions of who they already are.

But the pattern is not limited to children who visibly struggle. External validation can shape identity just as powerfully when a child is succeeding. The child who tries hard but struggles may begin to believe, “No matter how hard I try, I am not enough.” The child in the middle may learn that effort only matters when it makes them exceptional. The child praised for being smart, talented, attractive, popular, or athletic may begin to confuse achievement or status with worth. Some learn that they are less than. Others learn that they are more than. Neither is the same as learning who they are. This is one reason I am wary of how heavily we rely on grades, rankings, rewards, points, prizes, badges, trophies, and other forms of external motivation to get young people to engage. These tools are not inherently bad. They can provide structure, make learning enjoyable, and encourage participation. But when we depend on them too heavily, we risk teaching young people to look outside themselves for the reason to care. What do I get? How many points is this worth? Is this for a grade? Did I win? Did I beat everyone else? Those questions can crowd out more important ones: What am I learning? What am I capable of? What kind of challenge helps me grow? How do my choices affect other people? What responsibility do I have here? What kind of person am I becoming?

The anxious high achiever may need to discover that worth does not disappear when achievement does. The gifted child may need to learn frustration tolerance and how to persist when something is no longer easy. The athlete may need an identity larger than performance. The popular child may need to know who they are when no one is watching. The average student may need to understand that a meaningful life is not reserved for people who rank at the top. The children who appear to be doing everything right may be losing something too.

We sort young people constantly: good student, bad student, gifted, average, troubled, talented, motivated, lazy, college-bound, not college material, leader, problem. We act as though these categories describe the person, when often they describe only how that person is performing in a particular environment, under a particular set of conditions, at a particular moment in their development. I know this because my own life did not unfold in a neat, linear way. At fifteen, I believed I was too far gone. I could not see the life ahead of me. I could not see that I would earn a GED, begin in remedial math at community college, graduate magna cum laude, earn a master’s degree with a 4.0, work in science and conservation, teach students, raise children, or eventually bring so many seemingly disconnected parts of my life together into one larger body of work.

I could not see around the corner.

And I was lucky.

That is part of my story too, and I do not want to tell it in a way that makes everything that came later sound like a simple triumph of determination. I received a scholarship. I happened to be dating a boy whose family helped me get a job and encouraged me toward community college. Those circumstances mattered. They opened doors that might not have opened otherwise.

Luck will always be part of life. Human relationships, timing, geography, money, health, opportunity, and chance all shape what becomes possible for us. But the luck itself was not what changed me. Plenty of young people grow up with money, excellent schools, influential families, tutors, coaches, connections, second chances, and nearly every conceivable advantage and still struggle with anger, entitlement, anxiety, apathy, disconnection, or a lack of direction. Sometimes privilege does not solve the problem. It simply makes it easier to hide.

What those lucky circumstances gave me was an opening. They created enough space for me to begin seeing something for myself. People had told me I had potential. They had probably told me my whole life was ahead of me. But I did not believe them. Another person can tell you that you have potential a hundred times, but that does not mean you can see it. When there is no evidence of possibility from where you are standing, encouragement alone may not be enough.

Little by little, I began to catch a glimmer of possibility on my own. Maybe my life was not over. Maybe I was not finished. Maybe there was somewhere else to go from where I stood. It was small at first. But it was mine.

That distinction matters deeply to me. We cannot eliminate luck from life or guarantee every young person the same family, opportunities, resources, relationships, or second chances. But we can be more intentional about creating space in which a person has the chance to notice something for themselves: a pattern, a choice, a strength, a new direction, or even one small piece of evidence that says, “Maybe there is somewhere else to go from here.”

That is the kind of space Trailhead is meant to create: room for a young person to discover something for themselves rather than relying on another adult to assign them hope, purpose, identity, motivation, or direction. Encouragement matters, and external support matters, but hope handed to us from the outside is not the same as hope discovered from within.

Trailhead creates room for that discovery: space to notice, reflect, see patterns, recognize choice, understand how you work, discover what gives you energy and what drains it, and recognize that the story you have accepted about yourself may not be the whole story. Lasting movement begins when we can finally see even the smallest possibility for ourselves.

And this is where the beauty of life comes in. Even when life is messy, nonlinear, confusing, or painful, we are still on our path. We are still gathering information, making choices, and learning what strengthens us, what diminishes us, what calls us forward, and what no longer fits. We do not have to find our true path somewhere out there. Our path is under our feet. We are creating it with every step we take.

That does not mean every step is wise, that circumstances do not matter, or that every hardship is secretly a gift. It does not mean people can simply choose their way out of injustice, illness, poverty, trauma, or loss. Agency is not the same as control. We do not control everything that happens to us. But within the reality of our circumstances, there are still moments when we can notice, choose, respond, learn, turn, begin again, or take one step in a different direction.

Sometimes that perspective allows us to loosen our grip on striving. To stop frantically trying to become the person we think we are supposed to be. To stop believing there is one correct path hidden somewhere in the distance that we must find before time runs out. To stop treating every wrong turn as proof that we have failed. To relax enough to pay attention.

What conditions help me thrive? What patterns keep appearing? What gives me energy? What drains it? What have I learned from the places where I struggled? What parts of myself have I been trying to erase when they may actually need understanding and direction? What matters enough to me that I keep returning to it? What becomes possible when I take responsibility for my choices without confusing my mistakes with my identity? These are not small questions. They are the questions underneath a life.

And perhaps that is the pattern we have been missing. We have spent so much time trying to get people to perform, comply, achieve, choose, and succeed that we have not given them enough space to understand themselves as people in development. We rush young people toward answers before they have learned how to ask questions about themselves. We tell them to choose a direction before helping them understand how they move through the world. We try to motivate them from the outside before giving them enough space to discover what might begin moving them from within.

Trailhead begins there. Trailhead helps people become more conscious participants in their own lives by giving them space to better understand their patterns, strengths, vulnerabilities, motivations, needs, and choices. Agency matters. Responsibility matters. Self-understanding matters. And every person deserves the chance to recognize that no single moment, mistake, label, score, achievement, failure, advantage, disadvantage, or season of life tells the whole story of who they are becoming.

Sometimes a life begins to change because a door opens. Sometimes because of a person, a relationship, an opportunity, a moment of timing, or simple luck. What matters is what becomes possible in the space that opening creates. A person begins to see something they could not see before. A possibility. A choice. A different direction. Maybe even the first glimmer of hope they can finally see for themselves.
